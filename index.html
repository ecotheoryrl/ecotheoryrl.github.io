In reinforcement learning (RL), designing general-purpose algorithms that apply to arbitrary Markov Decision Processes (MDPs) is very appealing because it broadens the range of problems that we can address using this technique. However, when we utilize these methods to solve real applications, we put considerable time into carefully parameterizing the problem, such as selecting the appropriate state representations and action spaces, fine-tuning reward functions, and designing data collection strategies. RL is not alone in this regard: researchers in the supervised learning community typically assume datasets to be fixed (and iterate on the algorithms and models), while practitioners often fix the algorithm and model (and instead iterate on the dataset). Some have argued that perhaps a more data-centric view of machine learning research is needed [18, 12], and we would like to encourage the research community to investigate this same principle in the context of RL.

Data in RL may be understood to be the properties of environments and tasks, usually modelled through underlying MDPs. From this perspective, a data-centric study of RL would parallel Gibsonâ€™s ecological theory of visual perception and psychology [9]. An ecological study of RL should examine the behavior of algorithms in the context of their environment to further understand how different properties (such as linearity, ergodicity, mixing rate, among others) influence the performance of these methods. We want the community to develop a systematic approach to RL task design that complements the algorithmic-centric view that exists today. Properties and taxonomies of environments and tasks have been previously investigated in several areas of RL research such as curriculum and continual learning [27, 5, 22], bisimulations and homomorphisms [21, 7, 4, 26], PAC analysis [13, 1], information theoretic perspectives [14, 11, 16, 8], meta-analysis of RL benchmarks [17, 20, 19], among many others. However, these endeavors have been usually disconnected from the efforts made to build environments and tasks [2, 25, 3, 24, 23, 6, 15, 10] leaving a gap in our understanding on how algorithmic solutions and environments designs interact.

This workshop builds connections between different areas of RL centered around the understanding of algorithms and their context. We are interested in questions such as, but not limited to:

  1. How to gauge the complexity of an RL problem
  2. Which classes of algorithms can tackle which classes of problems
  3. How to develop practically applicable guidelines for formulating RL tasks that are tractable to solve
We expect submissions that address these and other related questions through an ecological and data-centric view, pushing forward the limits of our comprehension of the RL problem.

We encourage submissions that propose approaches to these questions that are relevant to areas including (but not limited to):

  1. Properties and taxonomies of MDPs
  2. PAC analysis of RL
  3. Information theory in RL
  4. RL benchmarks and their meta-analyses
  5. Skills Discovery
  6. Curriculum learning
  7. Continual learning
  8. Curiosity, intrinsic motivation and unsupervised RL
  9. Representation learning
  10. Exploration
  11. Human preferences
  12. Real-world applications of RL (Robotics, Recommendation, etc.)
  13. Novelty search, diversity algorithms, and open-endedness
  14. Dynamical systems and control theory
